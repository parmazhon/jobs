<h2 id="candidate">How candidates should prepare</h2>

Choose six stories before the interview:

1. A customer or cultural insight that changed the work
2. A concept or brief you are proud of
3. A successful creative test and what you learned
4. A failed test and what you changed afterward
5. A disagreement with a creative, media, client, or brand partner
6. A time you had too many requests and set priorities

For each, note the context, your responsibility, the evidence available, your decision, what collaborators did, the result, and what you would change. Keep the first answer to two or three minutes; let the interviewer request more detail.

Study the employer without building an unsolicited rebrand. Understand the customer, products, business model, channels, and visible creative patterns. Prepare thoughtful questions about the team’s workflow, output, production resources, measurement, and decision rights.

<h2 id="questions">24 creative strategist interview questions</h2>

### Customer research and insight

1. Walk me through how you learn about a new audience or product.
2. Tell me about an insight that materially changed your creative direction.
3. How do you distinguish an insight from an observation?
4. Which research sources do you trust, and how do you handle conflicting evidence?

**Listen for:** specific sources, synthesis, customer language, commercial relevance, and an insight that leads to a decision.

### Angles and concepts

5. What is the difference between an angle, a concept, and a hook?
6. Show us how you developed several distinct ideas from one customer problem.
7. How do you decide whether an idea is on-brand without making it predictable?
8. Describe a concept you decided not to pursue and why.

**Listen for:** clear definitions, strategic range, prioritization, and awareness of execution—not a list of trends.

### Briefs, copy, and creative feedback

9. What must be in a useful creative brief?
10. Show us a brief and explain which parts mattered most to the maker.
11. How do you write and evaluate hooks?
12. Tell me about feedback that improved the work without taking it over.

**Listen for:** a single-minded message, evidence, hypothesis, proof, platform context, constraints, and respectful collaboration.

### Performance and testing

13. Which creative metrics do you use, and what can each one tell you?
14. An ad has a strong hook rate but weak conversion. What would you investigate?
15. Describe a creative that failed. How did you interpret it and what did you test next?
16. How do you decide whether to iterate an execution, revisit the angle, or move on?

**Listen for:** diagnostic thinking, appropriate caution, funnel awareness, and a concrete next test. Be wary of anyone who treats correlation as certainty.

### Platform and production judgment

17. How would you adapt one idea across Meta, TikTok, and YouTube?
18. How do production constraints affect your strategy?
19. When is a polished execution better than creator-style content, and when is it not?
20. How do you maintain brand quality while increasing creative volume?

**Listen for:** channel-specific behavior, practical tradeoffs, and respect for production partners.

### Collaboration and ownership

21. Tell me about a disagreement with a media buyer, creative director, or client.
22. How do you prioritize when every stakeholder believes their request is urgent?
23. What does a good weekly creative review look like?
24. What would you aim to understand and improve in your first 90 days here?

**Listen for:** clear communication, curiosity, decision principles, documentation, and goals that fit the role’s actual authority.

<h2 id="portfolio">Portfolio review questions</h2>

Do not ask a candidate to narrate an entire portfolio. Select one case and trace the chain of reasoning:

- What was the business and customer problem?
- What evidence did you have, and what was missing?
- What was the key insight?
- Which alternatives did you consider?
- What did you personally decide, write, or make?
- Who else contributed?
- How did the execution change from the original brief?
- What happened after launch?
- What can the results reasonably tell us?
- What would you make next?

Candidates should bring artifacts when possible: research synthesis, a messaging framework, test roadmap, brief, script, feedback, and results view. Confidential details can be redacted. The [portfolio guide](/guides/creative-strategist-portfolio-guide/) explains what a complete case should contain.

### A fair work sample

If a work sample is necessary, give every finalist the same fictional or retired brief and the same 60–90 minute limit. Ask for a lightweight output such as:

- Three audience or message hypotheses
- Two distinct concepts
- One brief outline
- The first test and the expected learning
- Important questions or assumptions

Pay candidates for their time. Do not ask them to solve an active business problem or produce launch-ready ads for free. Score the thinking, prioritization, clarity, and awareness of limitations—not visual polish unless design is part of the job.

<h2 id="scorecard">Creative strategist interview scorecard</h2>

Rate each competency from 1 to 5 and require written evidence.

| Competency | 1: Limited evidence | 3: Capable | 5: Exceptional |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Customer insight | Repeats broad demographics | Finds usable patterns | Uncovers specific tensions that reshape the work |
| Strategic judgment | Generates without prioritizing | Makes a sound, supported choice | Balances customer, brand, channel, and business tradeoffs |
| Concepts and copy | Produces surface variations | Develops distinct executable ideas | Creates strategic range with sharp message craft |
| Performance learning | Reports metrics | Forms plausible diagnoses | Connects evidence to a rigorous next test without overclaiming |
| Collaboration | Describes individual output | Works effectively across functions | Improves decisions and work across the whole system |
| Communication | Difficult to follow | Clear and organized | Concise, persuasive, and adaptable to the audience |

Decide the required score before interviews begin. A writing-heavy individual contributor and a client-facing director should not use identical weights. Compare evidence in a structured debrief before discussing “culture fit,” which can easily become a proxy for familiarity.

Candidates can also use this rubric: identify the weakest evidence in your portfolio and prepare a real example that demonstrates growth.
