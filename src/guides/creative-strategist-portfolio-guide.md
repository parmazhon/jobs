<h2 id="include">What belongs in a creative strategist portfolio?</h2>

A hiring manager is trying to answer three questions:

1. Can this person find a meaningful problem or opportunity?
2. Can they turn it into direction that produces good creative?
3. Can they evaluate what happened and improve the next decision?

Your portfolio should make those answers visible. Include:

- A one-paragraph introduction describing your specialty and experience
- Two to four relevant case studies
- Selected research, frameworks, briefs, scripts, or testing plans
- Representative final creative with context
- Results and learning where available
- Clear labels for your contribution and collaborators
- A resume or profile link and simple contact method

Do not open with a long personal manifesto. Help the reviewer understand your fit, then get to the work.

### Choose cases for range, not volume

Two complete cases are stronger than eight shallow galleries. Select projects that reveal different strengths—for example, one research-heavy launch and one performance iteration program, or one in-house brand and one agency client.

Lead with the case most relevant to the job. If the employer needs paid-social strategy for a consumer brand, do not make them begin with an unrelated organic rebrand simply because it looks better.

<h2 id="case-study">Creative strategy case-study structure</h2>

Use this sequence for each project.

### 1. Context and problem

In two or three sentences, identify the product, audience, channel, business situation, constraints, and goal. Replace vague goals such as “increase engagement” with the actual decision the team needed to make.

### 2. Your role

State what you owned, the project length, and who worked with you. For example: “I led review mining, angle development, briefs, and weekly analysis alongside a media buyer, editor, and two creators.” This builds more trust than implying you made everything.

### 3. Evidence and insight

Show the few research inputs that changed your thinking. Then write the insight as a tension or motivation with strategic consequence. A page of screenshots is not synthesis; explain what the pattern meant.

### 4. Strategic decision

Present the audience, angle, message hierarchy, offer or proof, and hypothesis. Explain why you chose this direction over plausible alternatives.

### 5. Concepts and briefs

Show enough of the brief, script, storyboard, or testing roadmap to demonstrate how the strategy became executable. Make small text readable and add a typed summary rather than relying on a screenshot of a dense document.

### 6. Execution

Include a few representative ads, frames, or links. Explain any meaningful changes between the brief and final work. Give collaborators credit.

### 7. Results and learning

Show only metrics you have permission to share. Provide a baseline, period, spend or sample context when relevant, and avoid implying that creative alone caused every business outcome. The most important line is often: “Based on this, we made…”

### 8. Reflection

End with what you would do differently now. Good strategists are expected to update their judgment; honest reflection is a strength.

<div class="guide-note"><strong>Keep it concise:</strong> Aim for roughly 6–10 focused slides or a web page that can be scanned in three minutes and discussed for twenty. Put supporting detail behind optional links.</div>

<h2 id="no-results">Building a portfolio without performance data</h2>

Many candidates cannot share client metrics, and aspiring strategists may not have launched work yet. You can still show credible thinking.

### If the work is confidential

Redact the brand, exact figures, audience size, or proprietary dashboards. Use indexed results where permitted—for example, “1.3× the account baseline”—and explain the limitation. Ask your employer or client before publishing anything uncertain.

### If you worked on organic or brand creative

Show the relevant strategic skills: research, positioning, concept development, briefing, stakeholder alignment, and learning. Do not force performance terminology onto work that was not measured that way.

### If you have no client work

Create a transparent self-directed case:

1. Choose a real product and a narrow channel or growth problem.
2. Research public reviews, comments, competitor ads, and category language.
3. Synthesize a specific insight and messaging opportunity.
4. Develop several concepts and one detailed brief.
5. Create rough storyboards or collaborate on sample executions.
6. Propose a test plan and explain what each result would teach you.

Label it **speculative** or **self-directed**. Never fabricate results, quotes, access to internal data, or a client relationship.

<h2 id="checklist">Portfolio review checklist</h2>

Before sending your portfolio, confirm that:

- The link opens without requesting permission.
- The site or PDF works on a phone and loads quickly.
- Your name, specialty, location or work authorization if relevant, and contact details are easy to find.
- Every case states the problem, your contribution, and collaborators.
- Research is synthesized into an insight.
- Concepts are distinct strategic ideas, not only hook variations.
- At least one brief or equivalent direction artifact is visible.
- Results include context and do not overstate causation.
- Confidential and speculative work is labeled honestly.
- There are no broken video, ad-library, or prototype links.
- The first case matches the role you are applying for.
- A reviewer can understand the main story in under five minutes.

### Common portfolio mistakes

Avoid giant ad galleries, tiny deck screenshots, generic process diagrams, unexplained metrics, and password gates with no password in the application. Remove school-style definitions of common terms unless they are necessary to understand your decision.

Finally, practice presenting one case without reading the slides. The portfolio gets you into the conversation; your ability to explain tradeoffs and learning often determines what happens next.
